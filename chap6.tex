\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

Designing useful visualizations for event sequences is challenging because of the persistent tension between representativeness and interactivity. As highlighted in~\autoref{chap:intro}, visualizations that aim to represent all events sacrifice interactivity, whereas systems designed for interactivity risk discarding or misrepresenting events. To ease the tension, in this dissertation, we claim \textbf{``useful visual solutions require treating visual design considerations and data management strategies as interconnected components, rather than separate concerns.''} This chapter provides discussions on how an interconnected visual design and data management strategies make useful visualizations for event sequences by easing the tension between representativeness and interactivity.


\section[Connecting Visual Design Considerations with Data Management Strategies]{Connecting Visual Design Considerations with \\ Data Management Strategies}

In this section, we discuss considerations for connecting visual design with data management strategies that emerged from our experiences and learned lessons. We synthesize the lessons learned across our studies and implementations, emphasizing how data abstraction, query optimization, and visualization strategies collectively influence interactivity and representativeness. We also discuss the limitations of a connected approach, acknowledging that while it can yield powerful, tailored solutions, it may reduce generalizability and require careful balancing of design goals.

\subsection{Relating Visual Data Abstraction with Data Formats}
\label{subsec:abstraction_format}

A fundamental requirement that emerged inevitably during our work is the need to consciously relate the \textit{visual data abstraction} chosen for a visualization design to the \textit{data format} used to store and transmit that data across system components. A data abstraction is the mapping of raw domain data to an abstract data type suitable for visualization~\cite{munzner2014vad}. For example, representing a set of pairwise relationships as a graph, or a sequence of timestamped occurrences as an ordered list of events. A \textit{visual data abstraction} refers to the data abstraction used directly for visual encoding. A \textit{data format} is the concrete structural and syntactic encoding in which data is physically held and exchanged between system components, such as the visual rendering layer, a data query manager, and the underlying databases~\cite{kandel2011wrangling}. Visualization designers often treat visual data abstraction and data format as separate concerns~\cite{bigelow2020guidelines}. Visualization systems define their own internal data abstractions tied to visual marks, while databases and backends impose their own storage formats. 

When the data abstraction assumed by the visual design and the data format used by the data management components are poorly aligned, two categories of problems arise. First, data may be \textit{misrepresented}, values that are semantically continuous may be rendered as discrete, hierarchical relationships may be flattened and lost, or missing entries may be silently misinterpreted. Second, the system incurs unnecessary \textit{data conversion overhead}, every component boundary between a format-native store and an abstraction-native renderer requires a translation step, increasing latency, introducing potential for error, and complicating maintenance~\cite{battle2020structured, heer2023mosaic, yang2024optimizing, kandel2011wrangling}. These problems create a persistent risk of a mismatch between visual data abstraction and data formats. Recognizing and resolving this mismatch early in the design process is necessary to maintain interactivity and representativeness.

We notice this mismatch during the progression of our work across the Traveler and ESeMan. In Traveler, we utilized a \textit{linear data format} for visualization where each element of the linear structure maps into a visual mark: one to a darker box, zero to no box, and any value in between to a gray box. While storing the data, we utilized an \textit{interval tree}. Therefore, prior to rendering the data, it was required to convert the interval tree into a linear structure to maintain our existing design choices. Later, we adopted a \textit{summed area table} that gives a significant boost to the interactive performance, but that sacrifices accuracy. Our contribution with ESeMan adopted a \textit{hierarchical data structure} to control the accuracy, which also helps to tune the interactive performance by balancing the visual accuracy. Crucially, the hierarchy was designed to mirror the visual data abstraction, each level of the hierarchy corresponded to a distinct granularity of visual representation, so that data fetched at a given level arrived in a form that required minimal conversion before rendering. The result was a measurable reduction in data fetch time, a reduction in inter-component conversion cost, and the preservation of semantically important event attributes such as start time and end time that had been lost or approximated in earlier format choices.

To apply this lesson in practice, we recommend treating \textit{visual data abstraction} and \textit{data format} as two design decisions that should be formalized together. Visualization designers should iteratively refine the data format and the visual data abstraction until these closely relate to one another.

To formalize a \textit{visual data abstraction}, engaging with collaborators and domain characterization~\cite{munzner2009nested, Sedlmair2012} are essential, since the relevant abstraction is often latent and not immediately apparent~\cite{bigelow2020guidelines}. When data is unfamiliar and not formally defined, conduct \textit{data reconnaissance}~\cite{crisan2019uncovering}, making an exploratory pass through the unfamiliar data, and \textit{task wrangling}~\cite{crisan2019uncovering}, progressively revising task descriptions as collaborators' needs and data availability change. When data is available, follow a \textit{data-first} strategy~\cite{oppermann2020data} by grounding the abstraction in what the data can actually support rather than deriving data requirements from an idealized design.

To formalize a \textit{data format}, profile the available data formats by documenting structural organization (such as flat tables, nested hierarchies, or multi-table collections~\cite{kasica2020table}) and enumerating attribute types and value ranges~\cite{kandel2011research}. Additionally, trace how data flows between system components like databases, query managers, and rendering layers~\cite{battle2020structured, yang2024optimizing}.

Next, compare the abstraction with the format and identify mismatches. These mismatches may be syntactic (different field names or encodings), structural (tabular data that must be converted into hierarchies or graphs), or semantic (same meaning different names or same name different meanings). Once identified, address the mismatch between abstraction and format by modifying the visual design, data management strategy, or both. For example, the visual design of Traveler requires a hierarchical abstraction but the data was stored in a linear format. We restructurred the data into a hierarchy before it reaches the visual component. Conversely, if the data format is fixed and cannot be changed, adjust the visual design to accommodate the format. For example, use a tabular visual abstraction for SQL backends.

Additionally, involve domain collaborators to prioritize certain visual data abstractions or data formats over others. However, collaborators can have diverse mental models of abstractions or formats for the same data~\cite{williams2023data, bigelow2020guidelines}. When data formats and visual data abstractions are hard to relate due to practical constraints or collaborators' requirements, utilize intermediate data transformation layers to convert between formats and abstractions. 

\subsection{Designing and Optimizing Queries Based on Visual Tasks}
\label{subsec:query_tasks}

A critical step in building event sequence visualization systems is to first identify the visual tasks that users need to perform. This principle resonates with Munzner's~\cite{munzner2009nested} widely adopted nested model of visualization design, in which characterizing domain tasks is the uppermost, and most foundational, level, with errors there inevitably cascading to every downstream design decision, including data abstraction and algorithm selection. Establishing the task space, therefore, provides a foundation for determining which queries are most important and where optimization efforts will have the greatest impact.

Recognizing that a single query can support multiple visual tasks creates opportunities to optimize both the data management and the visual interface. Recent works~\cite{heer2023mosaic, yang2024optimizing, vieth2023manivault} demonstrate that decoupling data processing from visual specification, where each view publishes its data needs as declarative queries that are then managed and automatically optimized by a central coordinator, yields scalable performance across multiple linked visualization components simultaneously.

Moreover, optimizing shared queries that serve multiple visual tasks provides compounded benefits, improving both interactivity and cross-view consistency. When a single query endpoint underlies multiple views, optimizing that query raises the performance of all views simultaneously, rather than requiring per-view tuning. It also ensures that all coordinated views operate from a shared data representation, which prevents subtle discrepancies that can otherwise arise when views issue separate, independently constructed queries~\cite{battle2020structured, vieth2023manivault}.

For example, in Traveler, both the Utilization view and the Gantt view are supported by the same query endpoint. Utilizing the same query endpoint allows the system to compute and fetch data while simultaneously serving multiple visual components. As a result, interactivity is improved because the same optimized query serves multiple needs, and consistency is maintained because all views are based on a shared data representation. More generally, designing queries around identified visual tasks enables visualization systems to avoid redundancy in data retrieval, reduce discrepancies between coordinated views, and focus optimization efforts on the queries that matter most to visualization users.

To apply this lesson in practice, after identifying the visual tasks using an established task taxonomy~\cite{brehmer2013multi, zinat2024multi}, group tasks by shared data requirements. For example, tasks drawing on the same entities, time ranges, or aggregation granularity are candidates for a shared query endpoint~\cite{heer2023mosaic, battle2020structured}. Next, design parameterized query endpoints scoped to each task cluster rather than to individual views~\cite{moritz2019falcon, heer2023mosaic}. Concentrate optimization efforts (indexing, pre-aggregation, and caching, etc.) on the endpoints serving the most frequent or latency-sensitive tasks first, as misaligning optimization strategy with the actual task profile is a primary cause of poor interactive performance~\cite{battle2020structured, moritz2019falcon}. Finally, validate that all views sharing a query endpoint remain mutually consistent and meet latency thresholds across the full range of supported interactions.

\subsection{Visible Pixels Aware Data Abstraction}
\label{subsec:pixel_abstraction}

Another important design consideration is to anchor the visual data abstraction in the visible pixel space. A system can avoid unnecessary computation on data that cannot be distinguished at the current resolution. We adopt a \emph{pixel window} approach, where the visible space is divided into pixel windows. Data management strategies are then adopted to fetch only the data necessary for these pixel windows.

The adoptation of a pixel window strategy provides two key benefits. First, it bounds the amount of data that must be processed to the display resolution, ensuring that interaction cost depends on the number of pixels rather than the total number of events. Second, it allows multiple summarization techniques, such as aggregation or sampling, to be naturally framed in terms of pixel windows, making results directly interpretable within the visual interface. In practice, the pixel window strategy guided the design of both our query layer and supporting data structures, ensuring that interactivity is maintained even at large scales.

The principle of pixel-aware abstraction has been highlighted in prior visualization systems, such as imMens~\cite{liu2013immens}, Falcon~\cite{moritz2019falcon}, and Nanocubes~\cite{lins2013nanocubes}, all of which demonstrate that performance can be decoupled from raw data size by constraining queries to the visible resolution. Our work extends the pixel-aware abstraction principle to event sequence visualization, where queries aligned with pixel windows allow interactive navigation of timelines while preserving representativeness at the visual level. The pixel-based framing exemplifies the central thesis of the dissertation: useful visual solutions require connecting visual designs with data management strategies, here by binding queries and data structures directly to the constraints of the visual interface.

\subsection{Balancing Representativeness and Interactivity}
\label{subsec:balance}

A central challenge in event sequence visualization arises from the need to satisfy both representativeness and interactivity~\cite{guo2021survey, ulmer2023survey}. Representativeness requires that the visualization accurately reflects the underlying data, while interactivity demands that the system responds quickly, within 100ms~\cite{battle2019role}, to user interaction. Achieving both simultaneously is difficult because ensuring representativeness often involves processing large volumes of data, which can lead to latency that undermines interactivity~\cite{battle2019role, moritz2017trust}. Conversely, optimizing for interactivity may require techniques that compromise representativeness~\cite{battle2020structured}.

In the context of exploratory analysis using event sequence visualizations, balancing between representativeness and interactivity remains a difficult challenge. Our developed system, ESeMan, demonstrates that maintaining a balance is possible for event sequences. However, in practice, designers often need to balance between representativeness and interactivity depending on user needs. One promising strategy is to provide options that allow users to control the balance explicitly. In our visual design, users control the data accuracy with the zoom level by scrolling and panning. Data queries are therefore designed to fetch specific data based on the zoom level and panning window. Although we didn't implement full progressive refinement~\cite{stolper2014progressive}, our system is designed to render an initial view quickly with approximate or partial data, and then refine the visualization with more accurate data as computation and data transfer complete. Our design ensures that the user receives immediate feedback, even if initially approximate, while progressively improving the representation. Visualizing all data at once is rarely useful for exploratory analysis; instead, we aim to present data that is useful for exploration while maintaining interactive responsiveness.

To find a balance in practice, identify the maximum delay users can tolerate before exploration is disrupted. Progressive visualization research~\cite{zgraggen2016progressive} characterizes the challenge of finding a balance as a tradeoff between result accuracy (representativeness) and computation speed (interactivity), showing that completely blocking strategies (maximal representativeness but zero interactivity) significantly hinder user exploration compared to instantaneous or progressive approaches. Complementarily, Moritz et al.~\cite{moritz2017trust} show that users can still extract valid insights from approximate results as long as approximation quality remains above a minimum threshold. Therefore, also find the minimum representativeness required for the analytic task to be trustworthy.

Next, choose an appropriate tradeoff depending on task requirements and the context in which the visualization will be used. Richer et al.~\cite{richer2022scalability} provide a model of scalability that treats computation time delay and representativeness as effort that depends on problem size, available resources, and assumptions about the deployment context. In addition to this model, we recommend identifying a tradeoff empirically by measuring user task performance~\cite{lam2011empirical}, such as task completion accuracy or time, across different analysis tasks.

Next, the balance between representativeness and interactivity should be made user-controllable. Providing a customizable data granularity to control representativeness and interactivity has become a common strategy in scalable visualization systems~\cite{tao2019kyrix, chen2021pyramid, heer2023mosaic, ulmer2023survey, hografer2023tailorable}. In ESeMan, we introduced \emph{pixel window} parameter to control the data granularity. By explicitly acknowledging and managing the balance between representativeness and interactivity, designers can create visualizations that are useful for the exploration of large event sequences.

\subsection{Limitations of a Connected Approach}

While connecting visual design considerations with data management strategies brings substantial value, a connected approach has several limitations. One limitation is that a connected design often leads to highly tailored solutions, systems that are deeply optimized for a specific type of visualization or data structure. The considerations discussed in this section make a visualization system powerful within its intended domain but reduce its generalizability across different visualization contexts or datasets.

Additionally, a connected approach adds to the complexity of designing and implementing the interface. If visual design and data management are treated as separate concerns, then the designer is able to concentrate on optimizing each independently. For a connected approach, any decision in one component (such as in visual encoding) needs to be coordinated with decisions in another (like data indexing). As a result, the visualization system becomes less flexible to changes in user tasks and data.

\section{Visual Design Guidelines}

In this section, we propose visual design guidelines for creating useful event sequence visualizations by connecting visual design considerations with data management strategies. Then, we discuss how these guidelines address the design challenges identified in prior event sequence visualization research.


\subsection{Use Connected Multi-Level Data Abstraction}
\label{subsec:connected_multi_level_abstraction}

A visualization system is composed of multiple components, each serving a distinct role in the visualization pipeline from raw data to visual rendering. Components can manage data, render the visual interface, or occupy intermediate roles that mediate between these two ends. Each component maintains a data abstraction level, which defines how data is represented and structured within that component. Data abstraction levels vary across components, a data management component may operate on raw records, while a visual interface component works with visual interface-ready representations such as coordinates or colors. A connected multi-level data abstraction, our first guideline, ensures each data transformation~\cite{kandel2011research} between abstraction levels occurs at most once in the entire pipeline. Furthermore, data abstraction levels should be defined such that data can be directly mapped between adjacent components without requiring additional conversion.

A connected data abstraction has three concrete benefits. First, it eliminates redundant transformations, improving efficiency. Second, it prevents the common pitfall of treating data and visual abstraction as separate concerns~\cite{battle2020structured, zinat2023comparative}, ensuring that what appears on screen faithfully reflects how data is processed. Third, it preserves representativeness, users can clearly see whether they are viewing a summary or complete data, avoiding misinterpretation.

Several prior works~\cite{elmqvist2009hierarchical, liu2013immens, tao2019kyrix, gotz2019visual, kruchten2022vegafusion, heer2023mosaic} have explored how to connect abstraction levels in visualization pipelines. For event sequence data specifically, Gotz et al.~\cite{gotz2019visual} show that a predefined event-type hierarchy can drive dynamic aggregation at runtime, allowing the data layer to recalibrate its output in direct response to the user's current exploration depth. None of these approaches impose a strict limit on the number of transformations permitted between components. Any data transformation that occurs between components adds latency and risks misrepresentation.

Our evaluation using ESeMan highlights the necessity of a connected multi-level data abstraction with strict enforcement on the allowable number of data transformations. M4 optimization~\cite{jugel2014m4}, used by both Vega~\cite{kruchten2022vegafusion} and Mosaic~\cite{heer2023mosaic}, had degraded visual accuracy and longer latency compared to ESeMan in our experiments. The data transformation was required more than once, during the database query, sending the data to the visual interface, and rendering the data. The statistical sub-sampling configuration had slightly better latency with degraded accuracy than M4. In these cases, because of the disconnected abstraction levels, data is either omitted or approximated during the transformation. Rather than approximating the visual resolution later, ESeMan makes the pixel window a connecting anchor between abstraction levels that helps to omit data transformations, resulting in sub-100ms latency and better visual accuracy.

Enforcing a connected multi-level abstraction requires careful upfront alignment of abstraction levels across all components, which is non-trivial. Visualization designers are therefore encouraged to follow established visualization process and validation frameworks during system design. Traditional models~\cite{munzner2009nested, meyer2015nested} prioritized a ``downstream'' approach to process and validation where data abstraction is fixed before visual encoding and algorithmic decisions are made. For event sequences, data cannot be effectively abstracted before the visual and implementation constraints are fully realized~\cite{zinat2023comparative}. The ``downstream'' rigidity disconnects data abstraction from visual and implementation realities, risking data misrepresentation and reduced interactivity. 

To apply our first guideline in practice, define a connected multi-level abstraction iteratively, allowing the design to evolve as visual and implementation constraints become clearer. First, define a candidate abstraction level, then trace through the full pipeline and examine every adjacent component boundary for data transformations or format conversions. If any are found, revise the abstraction definitions on either side of that level boundary until data transformation is no longer necessary. Repeat until at most one transformation remains across the entire pipeline. In our work, the visualization component operates at pixel resolution and the storage component holds pre-indexed KD-tree node summaries. The single transformation, raw events into the hierarchical index, occurs once at preprocessing. At query time, the pixel window is passed directly to the tree traversal and results conforming to the visual data abstraction are returned directly to the renderer, with no intermediate conversion at any component boundary.

Connected multi-level data abstraction carries an inherent risk of being defined broadly to accommodate every possible query and visual task, which can lead to over-generalization. In the extreme case, a fully general abstraction degenerates into storing raw records at every level, negating the efficiency gains that the guideline was intended to provide. The second guideline is presented next to mitigate over-generalization.

\subsection{Prioritize Visual Tasks Before Optimizing Data Management}
\label{subsec:user_tasks}

The second guideline is to prioritize the visual tasks users must perform before committing to any data management strategy. The prioritization of tasks in visualization design has long been recognized. Shneiderman's information-seeking mantra~\cite{shneiderman2003eyes}, Munzner's nested model~\cite{munzner2009nested}, and Brehmer and Munzner's task typology~\cite{brehmer2013multi} all establish tasks 
as the anchor from which data representation decisions should derive.
% Aigner et al.~\cite{aigner2011visualization} and Schulz et al.~\cite{schulz2013design} demonstrate that the diversity of time-oriented tasks (comparison, pattern detection, anomaly identification) drives fundamentally different requirements for how temporal records must be indexed and aggregated. 

For event sequence analysis specifically, Zinat et al.~\cite{zinat2024multi} proposed a hierarchical classification of tasks that maps high-level analytical goals to concrete system actions. However, there are no empirical studies for event sequence visualization that systematically identify which tasks should be prioritized when resources are finite and not all can be equally optimized. Our Gantt chart task taxonomy~\autoref{chap:gantt-taxonomy} provides literature-based indication on which task to prioritize. We identified the most frequent visual tasks noticed in our surveyed papers and tracked two relevant data queries. Later, ESeMan's design demonstrates what visual task prioritized data management looks like in practice. With ESeMan, we optimized the data management strategy to prioritize the two queries relevant to the most frequent visual tasks, which resulted in sub-100ms latency for datasets with millions of events.

The consequence of ignoring task priorities is well documented in the database-driven visualization literature~\cite{battle2020structured, battle2019role, zinat2023comparative}. In Zinat et al.'s framework~\cite{zinat2024multi}, ignoring task priorities corresponds to choosing a technique before the intent is established. Additionally, in Traveler, we noticed that our collaborators' priorities shifted and evolved over time. Therefore, data management decisions made early would have been misaligned with tasks what proved critical later. Choosing a data management strategy without grounding the strategy in actual visual tasks hampers interactivity and representativeness. Our wrong choice of data management strategy in Traveler was later confirmed during the evaluation of ESeMan. We observed that the chosen data management strategy during Traveler's design, using a summed area table, performed worse than our KD-Tree-based strategy focused on visual tasks on both interactivity and representativeness.

To apply our second guideline in practice, at first identify user goals and the visual tasks that serve those goals. Subsequently, identify which tasks are most frequent and most latency-sensitive, and structure data management around those tasks. For example, in our Gantt chart task taxonomy~\autoref{chap:gantt-taxonomy}, we identified that retrieving events within a time and track range and conditional range are the most performed and latency-sensitive queries across the visual tasks. Therefore, in ESeMan, we optimized our data management strategy to prioritize these two queries, which resulted in sub-100ms latency for datasets with millions of events. Grounding our optimization strategy on visual tasks gave us a clear rationale for which queries should be efficient, what abstractions are needed, and where optimization efforts should be focused. Without the rationale, data management decisions risk over-optimizing for tasks that are rarely performed or under-supporting tasks that are critical to the analysis workflow.

Additionally, when applying the second guideline, designers should confirm that each data abstraction is anchored to a concrete set of visual tasks, ensuring that the abstraction identified by the first guideline is also a \emph{minimal} one, retaining only the data the visual tasks actually demand.
